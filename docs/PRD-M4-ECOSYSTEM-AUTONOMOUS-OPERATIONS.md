# PRD: M4 Ecosystem and Autonomous Operations

**Status:** Draft
**Last reviewed:** 2026-02-20
**Milestone window:** Post-M3

## 1. Milestone Intent
M4 expands AWS Swarm from a strong first-party platform into an ecosystem with safe third-party extensibility, bounded autonomous workflows, and enterprise-grade governance.

M4 should increase network effects and expansion revenue without compromising reliability and safety.

## 2. Positioning
If M3 is about persistence and coordinated value, M4 is about scale and leverage:
1. More creators can build on top of the platform.
2. More organizations can operate with governance confidence.
3. More workflows can run semi-autonomously with auditability.

## 3. Scope (Broad)
### 3.1 Ecosystem Layer
1. Third-party templates/tool packages with trust and quality controls.
2. Discovery and ranking that prioritizes proven outcomes.
3. Permission and capability scopes for extensibility.

### 3.2 Autonomous Workflow Layer
1. Bounded recurring workflows with explicit policies.
2. Human approval checkpoints for high-risk actions.
3. Failure handling and rollback paths that are operator-friendly.

### 3.3 Organization and Governance Layer
1. Org-level policy management and delegated administration.
2. Compliance and audit surfaces suitable for enterprise buyers.
3. Cost and usage governance at team/org hierarchy levels.

### 3.4 Economic Layer
1. Monetization model for ecosystem assets.
2. Clear creator incentives and platform take-rate strategy.
3. Enterprise packaging for governance and SLA requirements.

## 4. M4 Product Outcomes
1. Ecosystem contribution drives meaningful share of platform usage.
2. Autonomous workflows deliver measurable throughput gains with low incident rates.
3. Organization-level controls unlock larger, multi-seat deployments.
4. Economic model improves retention and expansion without raising support burden disproportionately.

## 5. Entry Criteria (from M3)
M4 should start only when:
1. M3 reliability and trust targets are sustained.
2. Memory and coordination controls are proven in production.
3. Operational tooling can support more external contributors and complex workflows.
4. Governance and audit primitives are mature enough to enforce ecosystem policy.

## 6. Success Metrics (Broad)
1. Percent of active avatars using third-party ecosystem assets.
2. Workflow completion rate for autonomous flows with policy compliance.
3. Enterprise expansion metrics (seat growth, account expansion, retention).
4. Incident rate and mean time to recovery for autonomous and third-party powered flows.

## 7. Risks
1. Ecosystem quality and trust risk.
2. Abuse and policy circumvention risk.
3. Operational complexity and support load growth.
4. Misaligned incentives between creators, operators, and platform.

## 8. Mitigation Principles
1. Progressive trust model for ecosystem publication and execution.
2. Strong policy enforcement and least-privilege capability scopes.
3. Approvals and auditability for irreversible or high-risk actions.
4. Gradual rollout with clear fallback modes and safety brakes.

## 9. Non-Goals (for initial M4 planning)
1. Fully permissionless ecosystem launch from day one.
2. Unbounded no-approval autonomy for sensitive workflows.
3. Parallel large-scale architecture rewrites not tied to M4 outcomes.

## 10. Open Strategic Questions
1. What ecosystem governance model best balances speed and trust?
2. Which workflow categories should require mandatory human approval?
3. What is the right initial marketplace monetization model?
4. Which compliance capabilities are minimum for first enterprise expansion wave?

## 11. Expected M4 Planning Outputs (at kickoff)
1. Ecosystem governance model v1 (publication tiers, trust levels, enforcement actions).
2. Autonomous workflow policy matrix (approval required vs optional by risk class).
3. Enterprise governance packaging outline (RBAC, audit, compliance baseline).
4. Monetization hypothesis doc with rollout experiments and guardrails.
